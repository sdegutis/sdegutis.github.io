Severe Imposter Syndrome Before working this morning, I decided to browse HN and came across the article [I built a garbage collector for a language that doesn't need one](https://claytonwramsey.github.io/2023/08/14/dumpster.html). Realizing it was about Rust, I immediately assumed the article would be beyond my comprehension to a large degree, and that I wouldn't be able to understand any code samples. The problem with this assumption is that *I have written hundreds if not thousands of lines of working Rust code and got paid for it.* This was about 4 years ago, and I don't remember a single thing about the project, or what kind of Rust I wrote. I don't *consciously* remember anything about the language. But I know from experience that, if I were to sit down and try to write some Rust with docs in hand, it would all come back to me relatively quickly, within hours. This also happened a few years ago, maybe 2018, when I was at a job interview for a Ruby position. Having not written any Ruby since 2012, I was convinced I forgot most of it. But I needed a job, and I knew I *had* written Ruby successfully in the past, so I went to the interview, and within minutes into the code challenge, my Ruby began to return in large chunks of knowledge at a time, until it was almost entirely restored. One way I can benefit from knowing this is realizing that *I probably have way more skills than I realize,* especially when it comes to software development. Since I can't rely on either my memory or my gut feeling to figure out what I'm capable of, the only other way would be for me to keep a list of my accomplishments around for me to refresh my memory with. Which means my portfolio here is just as much for me as for anyone else. Another way is to realize I can charge more than I am. Although unfortunately and immutable circumstances of my life prevent me from ever being able to charge the full amount for the work I'm (apparently highly) qualified to write, I'm still probably under-charging my clients. Obviously I won't raise the price for any existing ones who have taken a chance on me, but I should probably start with a higher asking price when talking with new clients. But it also means I have an *absolute need* to filter myself when talking to clients about potential projects. Rather than go based on my gut feeling, which is apparently broken, I have to research carefully what I'm capable of, before giving a quote or estimate. Otherwise, I might reject something I'm definitely qualified for, or on the other end of the spectrum, incorrectly assume I'm capable of something I'm really not. Overall, it feels like a win to realize that I'm capable of writing way more software than I thought I could. I wonder how common this experience is too, and I bet the general population probably underestimates their abilities more often than they realize, although maybe not to this degree.
