Ruby I'm liking Ruby's object system a lot. I love the concept of mixins and I love the way ruby instance methods/variables work. A lot of it is great. But Ruby has one huge difference from Python: Ruby is completely okay with there being N ways to accomplish a given task, with each solution being subtly different. Take the for..in feature for example. Most people I have asked tell me it's just syntactic sugar around list.each do..end, but it's not. ```ruby for a in [1,2,3] puts a end a #=> 3 ``` Versus: ```ruby [1,2,3].each do |a| puts a end a #=> NameError: undefined local variable or method `a' for main:Object ``` Personally the "N ways to do something" philosophy looks like a huge recipe for disaster. Fortunately most people I've asked about the differences told me to simply use the latter and avoid the former. Good solution, but it only masks the underlying problem, which is Ruby's error-prone philosophy.
