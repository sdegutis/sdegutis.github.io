Why I Code ### I'm not a real programmer, but an inventor. When I was probably near 8 years old, my dad began buying me kits from Radioshack to start making my own inventions. They weren't very expensive, and consisted mostly of a small motor, lightbulb, AA battery, some somewhat-elastic bands, and a popsicle stick. But man, the things you could do with these things seemed limitless! Until, of course, you hit a dead end. Well, only temporarily. My dad ended up getting me another kit that contained a magnet, a sensor, and a small (yet irritating) buzzer. The only use I could find for this extension of my collection, was as a door alarm, and so I hooked it up to our door. I remember how proud I was: I had made a genuine door alarm! The way it worked is: if I forgot to flip the switch to "off" and someone walked through the door and made that horrible buzzing sound, it signaled an alarm that let me know I'd better leave the area post haste before they caught me! Yes, this was not practical. I couldn't invent anything worth-while, partially for lack of ideas, and partially because we just couldn't afford all the little widgets required for good prototyping. Yet I loved the concept of making something practical out of simple materials. ### Programming is the Poor Man's Inventing I'm the kind of person who loves to get deep into the actual experimentation side of inventing. Reading books about the laws of physics and electromagnetism is interesting, and by no means am I claiming it's unnecessary, but actually pulling out a magnet and doing some fun little tests is just as necessary to help connect all the information together in your brain about how something works. Imagine having an infinite number of magnets of all sizes to test with, an infinite number of electronic circuits and circuit-boards and motors and servos and gears and whatever device you can dream of. And what you don't have, you can make a "prototype" of from what you do have, and create an infinite amount of copies of this new "invention-to-help-inventing". Well, we uniquely have that rare chance in this modern time: So let's pick up our NSStrings and wield our Xcode.apps, and get inventing! It's one of the best things we can do with our minds, and best of all, we never run out of materials!
