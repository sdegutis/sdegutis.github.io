---
title: "Leviathan and the App Store"
---

I'm making a Clojure IDE, codenamed Leviathan. I plan to sell it. But I probably won't sell it on the App Store.

A really important factor in this decision is Apple's Human Interface Guidelines (HIG). They retain the right and freedom to reject any app which they feel violates their HIG.

That's something I somewhat disagree about. Not enough to make any big public rants, or to boycott the App Store, but enough to avoid putting my apps there.

Even Apple doesn't even follow their HIG to the T in their own apps. Not because they're hypocrites. It's because the HIG is a living, breathing thing. Well, kind of.

The point of the HIG is that apps should be intuitive, offer a really great user experience, and act consistently with other apps you're already used to (when it makes sense to).

The HIG represents _us_, the human element. But the human element is always changing. And technology is always changing. And the way humans_interact_ with technology is always changing. That's what I mean when I say the HIG is a living, breathing thing. There's too many moving parts.

So I don't blame Apple for rejecting apps that don't follow the HIG. But my app is going to follow the _spirit_ of the HIG, not necessarily the letter. I'm working hard to make this a truly exceptional app. But I want the people using Leviathan to be the ones who help me decide what that means for it, not Apple.

That said, Leviathan is almost ready for a private beta, possibly any week now. If you're interested,email me. I'll write another post soon to describe its particular niche. But the short version is that it only understands Clojure, which allows it to be both super lightweight and extremely integrated.
